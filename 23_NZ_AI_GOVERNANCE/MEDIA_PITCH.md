## THE ONE-PARAGRAPH PITCH

A self-taught researcher in Dunedin has spent years building a mathematical framework for AI governance grounded in Māori epistemology — and is giving it away. The Lycheetah Framework includes four implementable AI accountability standards for Aotearoa New Zealand: an annual "Warrant of Fitness" for AI systems, a disclosure standard requiring AI to label what it knows versus what it's uncertain about (grounded in Te Ao Mārama / Te Ao Pō / Te Kore), a genealogical accountability standard based on whakapapa, and a Matariki annual audit. All open-source. No legislation required. And the framework maintains a public Failure Museum — a permanent record of every claim it got wrong. Nothing removed. Ever.

## THE ANGLES

### For technology journalists (RNZ, The Spinoff, Wired, MIT Tech Review)

**"The AI governance framework the EU doesn't have"**

The EU AI Act categorises risk. The Lycheetah Framework requires AI systems to account for whose knowledge built them and what they owe those people. One is a compliance checklist. The other is a constitutional architecture.

The specific claim: a government ministry could adopt the Community AI Warrant of Fitness tomorrow, by ministerial directive, without any new legislation. No other country has an AI governance standard grounded in indigenous epistemology that is simultaneously a formal mathematical framework and immediately implementable.

The tension: the person who built it is an independent researcher in Dunedin with no institution, no grant, no supervisor. Working from a 2012 laptop. The framework has an arXiv preprint and a public Failure Museum. It has not been peer-reviewed by a university. It may be the most rigorous AI governance work in the Southern Hemisphere. It may have errors. Both of these things can be true.

### For Pacific and indigenous affairs journalists

**"AI built on Māori terms"**

Tikanga Māori is not cultural decoration on a Western governance framework. The Lycheetah Framework proposes it as the constitutional ground from which AI governance for Aotearoa grows. The Three Worlds Disclosure Standard uses Māori cosmological structure — the known, the uncertain, the unknowable — as the epistemological architecture for AI transparency. The Whakapapa Disclosure requires AI systems to account for their ancestral knowledge debt the way a person accounts for whakapapa.

The framework is marked [PROPOSAL] on all te reo Māori encodings. They require iwi validation. The researcher is actively seeking that partnership. The honest admission that the work is incomplete without that relationship is part of the story.

### For NZ political journalists

**"The AI accountability standard a minister could adopt tomorrow"**

The question for the political desk: why hasn't it been adopted? The standards exist. The legal pathway exists (ministerial directive). The precedent exists (WOF for vehicles is already a national institution). What is blocking it?

The framework explicitly names the political pathway: the minister responsible for Science, Innovation and Technology, or the minister for Digitising Government, could direct one government agency to pilot the WOF in 90 days. The pilot would produce published results. The results would inform legislation — or demonstrate it isn't needed.

### For international journalists (Guardian, Al Jazeera, rest-of-world tech)

**"The country that could set the global standard"**

New Zealand is small. It has no economic leverage. It has something almost no other country has: a living constitutional relationship with indigenous governance traditions — actively contested, actively developed — that contains governance principles the rest of the world is only now discovering. Intergenerational thinking. Relational accountability. The standing of future generations in present decisions.

These are not soft values. They are load-bearing architecture. And one independent researcher in Dunedin has built a formal mathematical system that encodes them.

## WHAT MAKES THIS A STORY

Most AI governance stories are about what might happen. This one is about what exists and hasn't been adopted yet.

The gap between the standards existing and the standards being used is the story. The question of why serious, implementable work built without institutional backing sits unused while governments wait for overseas companies to self-regulate — that is the question this story asks.

The researcher is not asking for credit. The work is given away. The Failure Museum is the most unusual thing about it: a permanent, public record of every mistake, because the alternative — hiding errors — is the first step toward becoming the thing the framework argues against.

**"Reality has a vote. We publish our failures."**
