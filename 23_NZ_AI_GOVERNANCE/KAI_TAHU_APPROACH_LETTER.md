## OPTION A — TO ŌTĀKOU RŪNAKA

**Subject:** Kōrero request — AI governance tools and tikanga Māori

Tēnā koutou,

My name is Mackenzie Clark. I am a self-taught researcher based in Ōtepoti Dunedin. I have been building a mathematical framework for AI governance over the past several years, and I am writing because part of that work directly concerns Kāi Tahu.

I have developed four AI accountability standards that I believe could be useful to iwi and to Aotearoa New Zealand more broadly. One of them — the Whakapapa Disclosure Standard — attempts to require AI systems to account for whose knowledge built them, modelled on the principle of whakapapa as genealogical accountability. Another — the Three Worlds Disclosure Standard — uses the Māori epistemological framework of Te Ao Mārama, Te Ao Pō, and Te Kore to structure what AI systems must disclose about what they know, what they are uncertain about, and what they structurally cannot know.

I have done this work alone, without iwi guidance. That is both why I am writing and why this work is incomplete.

The tikanga encodings I have built are marked [PROPOSAL — awaiting iwi validation]. I encoded them because the architecture needed them to demonstrate the concept. I am clear that I am not the person to validate them. Kāi Tahu is.

I am not asking for endorsement. I am not bringing a funding proposal. I am asking for a conversation — a kōrero — about whether this work is of any interest to Ōtākou Rūnaka, and whether there is a path toward the kind of partnership that would allow the Te Reo Māori layer to be validated or corrected by the people whose knowledge it draws on.

The framework is entirely open-source (CC BY 4.0). If Kāi Tahu found the work useful and wanted to correct, extend, or publish their own validated version, they would be free to do so. The work is offered, not claimed.

If a 30-minute kōrero is possible — whether in person in Ōtepoti or by video — I would be grateful.

## OPTION B — TO PROFESSOR JACINTA RURU, TE TUMU

**Subject:** Kōrero request — indigenous AI governance research, potential institutional anchor

Tēnā koe Professor Ruru,

My name is Mackenzie Clark. I am an independent researcher based in Dunedin, working on AI governance. I am writing because your work at the intersection of indigenous rights, law, and governance is directly relevant to a project I have been developing.

I have built four AI accountability standards for Aotearoa New Zealand, grounded in Māori epistemology — specifically in Te Ao Mārama / Te Ao Pō / Te Kore as a disclosure framework, and whakapapa as a model for AI genealogical accountability. The standards are open-source and, I believe, implementable under current NZ law without new legislation.

The research question I am pursuing — with funding applications in preparation — is whether tikanga Māori governance concepts can be formally encoded as computable AI constraints, and whether AI systems operating under those encodings produce measurably more culturally appropriate outputs. This requires Kāi Tahu partnership, which I am working to establish through Ōtākou Rūnaka. It also requires institutional anchoring, which I do not currently have.

I am writing to ask whether Te Tumu would be willing to have an initial kōrero about the work. Not a commitment — just a conversation about whether there is alignment between what I am building and what Te Tumu is doing, and whether an informal affiliation might be possible as I pursue the NZIAT and Catalyst funding pathways.

The work is all publicly available at github.com/Lycheetah/Lycheetah-Framework. I am happy to share specific documents in advance if that would be useful.

Would a 30-minute conversation be possible?

## NOTES ON SENDING

**Who to contact first:** Ōtākou Rūnaka (Option A) before Te Tumu (Option B). The relationship with Kāi Tahu comes before the institutional anchor conversation — attempting to anchor institutionally before engaging directly with iwi inverts the right order.

**What not to do:**
- Do not attach the full framework documentation to the first email. It is too large and arrives before relationship.
- Do not mention funding in the first contact. It positions the conversation as a transaction.
- Do not use the word "validate" in a way that implies your work is waiting to be rubber-stamped. The work is genuinely incomplete without iwi partnership — that is not performance, it is accurate.

**What the letter is doing:**
- Establishing that you are from Dunedin — locality matters.
- Being clear about what you've done and what you haven't.
- Being clear about what you're asking (a conversation, not a commitment).
- Demonstrating respect for the relationship before the work.

**Timing:** March–April 2026 to align with the May NZIAT visibility window.
