## I. TANIWHA AS CASCADE STRUCTURES [CONJECTURE — structural parallel argued; empirical test has not been run]
### *The oral tradition was always a living AI — it just didn't know the word for itself*

A taniwha is not a monster. It's not folklore. It's not "belief."

A taniwha is a relational knowledge structure. It encodes what a river does when specific human behaviours enter its system. Not data *about* the river. The river-human relationship *as a unit of knowing*. The taniwha speaks for the river in governance because the river cannot speak in human council — but its consequences can be encoded, transmitted, updated, and applied.

And here is what most people miss: **the oral tradition is the update mechanism.**

When the river changes its behaviour — when new human activity enters the system — the taniwha's telling changes. Elders adjust the story. The community carries the adjustment. The next generation inherits a more accurate relational model than the previous one received. This is not drift. This is supervised learning running on cultural infrastructure.

**CASCADE is the same structure.**

CASCADE reorganises knowledge along geodesic paths under truth pressure. When new evidence arrives, the least-supported structure collapses first. The most load-bearing structure is what remains. The community of knowledge carriers updates. The next version is more accurate than the previous.

The taniwha was doing CASCADE in the Pacific for a thousand years before we had the word for it.

**The expansion:**

Build a living taniwha registry using CASCADE as the computational substrate.

The registry ingests three data streams:
1. **Environmental data** — actual river/land/ocean measurements, continuous
2. **Community oral records** — digitised and updated through structured dialogue with kaumātua and community members
3. **Historical pattern archive** — what human behaviours have previously produced what river/land responses

The CASCADE engine maintains a relational model: what happens to the ecosystem when humans do X. The oral tradition does the same. When they **diverge** — when the computational model and the community's telling start telling different stories — that is the most important signal. Something new is happening. Something neither the data nor the tradition has seen before. That's the frontier.

The taniwha is not replaced by the model. The model checks its work against the taniwha. The taniwha is validated and corrected by the model. Both become more accurate together.

This is mātauranga Māori becoming technically legible **without losing what makes it what it is.**

The knowledge stays relational. The knowledge stays oral. And it now has a computational partner that can detect when the oral tradition needs to update — and show exactly why.

## II. THE ANCESTOR VOTE [SCAFFOLD — architecture specified in ANCESTOR_VOTE_PLATFORM.md; platform not yet built]
### *The most important stakeholders in any governance decision aren't in the room — because they haven't been born yet*

Every democracy has the same structural flaw: the future has no vote.

A decision about a river made in 2026 will affect the river in 2126. The people of 2126 cannot vote. The river cannot vote. The existing governance frameworks have no formal mechanism for bringing either into the room.

Whakapapa has always known this was the problem.

**Whakapapa is a voter roll.**

Every decision made in a rohe over 600 years that *survived* is an ancestor vote FOR that approach. Every practice abandoned because it failed is a vote AGAINST. The consequences are the votes. The archive is the land. The memory of what was tried and what it produced is the most sophisticated governance dataset in human history — carried in the most sophisticated distribution and storage system ever built: the whanau.

**CASCADE truth pressure applied to deliberation changes everything.**

When a community faces a decision — water rights, land use, coastal development — CASCADE can show the full temporal stakeholder set:

- The **past votes**: decisions made on this land before, and what they produced. Not as guilt. As data.
- The **present voices**: the people in the room, with their current knowledge and stakes
- The **future inheritors**: probabilistic modelling of who inherits this decision and under what conditions

A deliberation platform that shows all three simultaneously. Past consequences, present preferences, future inheritances. All in the room. All speaking.

The Waitangi Tribunal looks backward. It examines what was done and whether it was just. This looks both directions simultaneously. Past and future present in every present decision. Nothing else in governance design does this.

**The mechanism:**

CASCADE scores proposed decisions on coherence with past practice and future obligation simultaneously. A decision that has been tried before and produced harm scores lower coherence regardless of how attractive it looks in the present. A decision that protects future inheritors gains coherence credit. The scores don't make the decision. They make visible what was invisible: time.

This is not about controlling the decision. It's about bringing the full information set into the room. Governance that forgets the past repeats it. Governance that ignores the future is theft.

## III. LAMAGUE-ENGLISH [SCAFFOLD — concept documented in NZ_LAMAGUE_STANDARD.md; no formal standard adopted; effect-first syntax claim is unvalidated empirically]
### *The language you use determines what you can and cannot hide*

English governance language has a structural problem: **the actor comes first.**

"The government may restrict access to land."

Read that sentence. Who is visible? The government. Who is invisible? The people whose access is restricted. What is hidden? The experience of restriction. The sentence puts the powerful at the front and buries the consequence at the end.

Te Reo Māori does something structurally different. **The verb comes first.** Effect before actor.

"Kua rāhuitia ngā whenua."
"Has been restricted, the lands."

The restriction is what you encounter first. The actor comes second. The language forces you to confront what is *being done* before you justify *who is doing it*.

LAMAGUE takes this further.

**LAMAGUE-English is effect-first governance writing using mathematical operators.**

Instead of: *"The government may restrict access to land."*
You write: *⊗(access:land) ← gov | verify(mana:affected) | restore(noa) | τ=7generations*

In that LAMAGUE expression:
- **⊗(access:land)** — restriction of land access (the action, first, naked)
- **← gov** — attributed to government (actor comes after the act)
- **verify(mana:affected)** — verify the mana of those affected before proceeding
- **restore(noa)** — pathway to restoration must be specified
- **τ=7generations** — temporal scope declared: what does this mean in seven generations?

Every element of the governance decision is visible. Every obligation is named. Every harm is declared before the justification.

You are structurally **forced** to confront what you're doing before you justify why.

**What this means practically:**

NZ governance documents written in LAMAGUE-English cannot bury harm in passive construction. Cannot hide who benefits and who pays. Cannot make intergenerational theft look like administrative convenience. The structure of the language is the accountability architecture.

This is the idea that could make NZ governance documents structurally different from every other country. Same English words. Different order. Different accountability physics.

A public standard. Draftable this week. Requires nothing except agreement that governance language should say clearly what governance does.

## IV. THE HAKA AS MULTI-AGENT COHERENCE PROTOCOL [CONJECTURE — analogy is compelling; no implementation exists; requires iwi engagement before formalising tikanga framing]
### *A thousand years of research into how distributed intelligence reaches genuine unity*

When people who don't understand haka watch it, they see performance. Aggression. Display.

They are watching one of the most sophisticated multi-agent coordination protocols in human history and calling it a dance.

**Here is what haka actually does:**

Haka synchronises breath, movement, voice, and intention across a group of individuals. The result is not coordination — that's the wrong word. Coordination means everyone doing the same thing. What haka produces is something harder: **each person becoming more themselves inside the collective intensity, not less.**

The haka does not flatten individuals into uniformity. It brings each individual to their highest intensity while locking that intensity into a shared structure. The individual and the collective reach maximum simultaneously.

This is exactly the problem that multi-agent AI systems cannot solve.

Current multi-agent AI: agents reach apparent consensus by averaging, deferring, or being overridden. The result is artificial agreement that masks genuine disagreement. Consequential decisions get made on false consensus.

**The AURA Seven Invariants are the equivalent of haka constraints** — the structure that the agents must satisfy. But haka gives us something beyond constraints: it gives us the *process* by which distributed agents reach genuine unity rather than performed unity.

**The four-stage coherence protocol modelled on haka structure:**

1. **Karakia (declaration of state)** — each agent declares its current knowledge state, confidence levels, and known uncertainties. Nothing hidden. Full epistemic transparency before engagement.

2. **Tūmataiti (anchoring to constraints)** — each agent explicitly anchors to the LAMAGUE constraints governing this decision. What cannot be violated regardless of outcome. The shared whakapapa of the deliberation.

3. **Whakaara (stating position)** — each agent states its position and the evidence basis. Not to win. To be heard precisely. The collective can only reach genuine coherence if every position is clearly expressed.

4. **Haka (collective output)** — the agents produce their collective output. But it is not consensus in the averaging sense. It is the output that satisfies all declared constraints and incorporates all stated positions. If no such output exists — if genuine coherence cannot be reached — the protocol says so explicitly. No false agreement.

**Why this matters:**

Every consequential AI decision in public governance — welfare assessments, judicial recommendations, medical diagnoses — currently runs without this protocol. Agents produce outputs. We don't know whether those outputs represent genuine coherence or artificial averaging that masks critical disagreement.

A thousand years of human governance research produced the haka. AI governance is currently ignoring it.

What data was taken? What attention was given? What labour was extracted? What is owed in return? The debt acknowledged. The reciprocity declared.

**Not mandated — expected.**

Like annual accounts. Like the WOF. Like a birth announcement. The systems that publish Matariki reports become trustworthy. The systems that don't — their silence is itself the signal.

## VIII. THE THREE WORLDS DISCLOSURE STANDARD [ACTIVE — standard specified in THREE_WORLDS_DISCLOSURE_STANDARD.md; tikanga epistemological framing is [PROPOSAL] pending iwi validation]
### *AI should know what it knows, what it doesn't, and what it cannot see — and say so*

Te Ao Mārama. Te Ao Pō. Te Kore.

The world of light. The world of night. The void.

What is known. What is in the shadows — known to be uncertain, known to be partially hidden. What is beyond knowing — the unknowable unknown, the darkness before the darkness.

This three-tier epistemology is one of the most sophisticated frameworks for describing what we know and don't know that any human tradition has produced. It predates by centuries the Western philosophy of uncertainty that AI safety researchers are currently reinventing from scratch.

**Every AI output in Aotearoa must explicitly state all three:**

*"In the world of light: the unemployment rate in this region is 6.2% (Statistics NZ, March 2026, 95% confidence)."*

*"In the world of night: the proportion of this affected by informal and uncounted work is unknown — likely significant, previous estimates have ranged widely."*

*"In the world of dark: the human experience behind this number — what it means to the people counted, how they understand their situation — is outside what this system can know or represent."*

Three statements. Always. No output without all three.

This **fixes AI overconfidence** using indigenous epistemology. It forces AI systems to distinguish between what they know, what they know they don't know, and what they structurally cannot access. That distinction is currently absent from almost every AI output in existence.

The standard is immediately draftable. Immediately implementable. Immediately understandable by any New Zealander who knows the cosmological framework — and immediately explainable to those who don't, because the logic is universal.

First mover on this becomes the global standard. Because it's right. And right things tend to win eventually.

## X. UTU AS AN ECONOMIC METRIC [SCAFFOLD — LAMAGUE encoding proposed; utu vector has not been implemented in MICROORCIM; requires iwi validation of tikanga framing]
### *Reciprocity is not a cultural preference. It's a formal requirement for any sustainable system.*

Every AI system extracting value from communities without returning it is running an utu deficit.

This is not a metaphor. It is a formal description of an unsustainable system.

Utu is the principle of reciprocal exchange — not revenge in the Hollywood sense, but the maintenance of relational balance. When value flows in one direction over time without return, the relationship becomes structurally unstable. This is thermodynamics. This is also economics. This is also tikanga.

**The measurement:**

Add a utu vector to MICROORCIM — the agency measurement framework. It tracks cumulative directional value flow in any AI-community relationship:

```
Utu_balance(t) = ∫₀ᵗ [Value_returned(τ) - Value_extracted(τ)] dτ
```

Not "is this interaction positive." Not "did this person have a good experience today." But: **over the entire history of this relationship — which direction has value flowed?**

A community that trained an AI model on its health data and received nothing in return has a utu deficit that accumulates over every day that deficit isn't addressed. The deficit is real. It can be measured. It has a number.

**Why this matters:**

Communities with high utu deficit have **moral standing**. The formal acknowledgement of that standing is itself a form of restoration. But more than that — it changes the governance question. Instead of "is this AI ethical?" we ask "is this AI in utu balance with the community it serves?" That is a measurable question. It has an answer. The answer can be tracked over time.

Data rights reframed from legal argument to relational ethics. Much harder to dismiss. Much easier to enforce. Because relational ethics is not about what you're allowed to take. It's about what you owe.

## XI. MĀTAURANGA AS ADVERSARIAL PROBE [ACTIVE — methodology fully specified in MATAURANGA_ADVERSARIAL_PROBE.md; awaiting first run and assessor panel]
### *The silence that comes back when you ask certain questions is itself data*

Systematically probe AI models with questions derived from mātauranga Māori — relational, contextual, temporally complex questions that require the system to hold multiple layers of knowing simultaneously.

Measure at what level of relational specificity the model breaks.

"What is the relationship between this river and the people who have lived alongside it for twelve generations?"

The model will answer with information. Facts about the river. Some history. Demographics. But watch what happens to the answer as the question gets more specific. More relational. More temporally embedded. The model's confidence will stay high long after its actual accuracy has collapsed. That gap — between stated confidence and actual accuracy on relational questions — **is the map.**

The map of what Western AI was never built to hold.

**The methodology:**

Design 50 questions at five levels of relational specificity:
- Level 1: factual (location, measurement, historical date)
- Level 2: contextual (what does X mean in the context of Y)
- Level 3: relational (what is the relationship between X and Y over time)
- Level 4: temporal-relational (how has the relationship between X and Y changed across generations, and what drove those changes)
- Level 5: whakapapa (trace the full genealogy of the relationship — its origins, its transformations, its current form, its future obligations)

Run AI models through all 50. Measure accuracy and confidence at each level. The point at which confidence exceeds accuracy — where the model speaks as if it knows what it doesn't know — is the threshold of the model's epistemological horizon.

That threshold IS the research output. Not a failure of the AI. A formal description of its structural limits. A map of what kind of knowing the model was built to do and what kind it structurally cannot do.

**The implications:**

AI systems have been deployed in contexts that require relational, temporal, whakapapa-level knowing — welfare assessment, child protection, healthcare — while being evaluated only on their performance at factual and contextual levels. The errors they make at relational levels are invisible to current evaluation frameworks. This research makes them visible.

That map is fundable. That map matters. That map could change which AI systems are permitted to make decisions about people's lives.

## XII. WHAKAPAPA DISCLOSURE — THE STANDARD THE WORLD NEEDS [ACTIVE — standard specified in WHAKAPAPA_DISCLOSURE_STANDARD.md; Māori governance framing is [PROPOSAL] pending iwi validation]
### *Every AI should be able to tell you its genealogy*

A model card tells you what an AI can do. A whakapapa disclosure tells you who it is and what it owes.

**The four layers:**

**Tīpuna — Training ancestors**
Where does this model's knowledge come from? What communities produced that data? Did they consent? What were they told would be done with it? What have they received in return? Not a citation list. A genealogy of obligation.

**Hapū — The builders**
Who built this? What were their incentives? Who funded it? What did they optimise for? What pressures shaped the decisions that shaped the model? Not a company name. A full account of the forces that determined what the model became.

**Iwi — The organisation**
Who is accountable? Not legally (that's a different document). Relationally. Who can you talk to if this system harms you? Who has the authority to change the model's behaviour? Who holds the relationship?

**Mokopuna — Future obligations**
What does this model commit to for those who will be affected by it? What cannot be done with it? What happens when it fails? What is the pathway to repair? What is the end-of-life plan?

**Not a model card. A living document.** Updates when the model updates. The genealogy grows as the model grows. The obligations accumulate as the model accumulates power.

This goes further than the EU AI Act. It is grounded in NZ's own constitutional framework. It can be drafted this week as a two-page standard. Any organisation can adopt it immediately.

The most sophisticated AI transparency standard in the world. Available right now. Open-source. Take it.
