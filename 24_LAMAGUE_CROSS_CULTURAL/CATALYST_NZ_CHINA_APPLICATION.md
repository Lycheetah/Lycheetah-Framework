## ABSTRACT (300 words — for submission cover)

The world's major AI governance frameworks operate within single philosophical traditions: the EU AI Act draws on Western liberal ethics; China's New Generation AI Governance Principles draws on Confucian-inflected state ethics; Māori data sovereignty frameworks draw on tikanga Māori. None of these frameworks can formally communicate with the others. None can demonstrate that their constraints are universally valid rather than culturally specific.

This research proposes to test a formally developed hypothesis: that the governance constraints encoded in Māori tikanga, Confucian philosophy, and Western formal ethics are structurally identical — independently discovered through millennia of real-world governance experience — and that LAMAGUE (Language of Alignment, Measurement, and Governance for Universal Ethics), a formal mathematical protocol developed at the Lycheetah Foundation, can serve as the shared language that makes their convergence testable, precise, and deployable in actual AI systems.

The research will proceed in three phases: formal convergence analysis (LAMAGUE encodings of Confucian governance concepts, developed with Chinese philosophy scholars); empirical validation (AI systems governed by single-tradition vs. cross-cultural LAMAGUE constraints, rated by representative user groups in NZ and China); and standard development (a LAMAGUE Cross-Cultural Governance Standard as the basis for a bilateral NZ-China AI governance protocol).

The NZ contribution is the Lycheetah Framework — nine formally developed frameworks including CASCADE (knowledge architecture), AURA (constitutional AI), and LAMAGUE (cross-cultural governance grammar), with thirteen Python implementations, a formal arXiv preprint, and 1,402 pages of documented development history. The Chinese contribution is the philosophical expertise and empirical research capacity to encode and test Confucian governance concepts using LAMAGUE. Together, the partnership produces the first formally validated cross-cultural AI governance standard — of direct relevance to every country attempting to build AI governance that is both technically rigorous and culturally legitimate.

## PART 1: RESEARCH CASE

### 1.1 The Problem

AI governance is fragmenting along cultural lines at exactly the moment it needs to converge.

The EU AI Act (2024) establishes a comprehensive governance framework — grounded in Western liberal rights theory. China's New Generation AI Governance Principles (2019) and subsequent Governance Framework (2023) establish a coherent governance tradition — grounded in Confucian collectivist ethics. Māori data sovereignty frameworks (Tino Rangatiratanga, 2019; Te Mana Raraunga, 2018) establish governance principles grounded in tikanga relational ethics.

All three frameworks are internally coherent. None can formally talk to the others. None can demonstrate that their constraints are universal rather than culturally specific. When an AI system operates across these jurisdictions — as all significant AI systems now do — there is no formal mechanism for determining which governance tradition takes precedence, or whether there is a shared foundation that all three traditions have independently discovered.

This is not a theoretical problem. NZ deploys AI systems from US and Chinese technology providers in public health, welfare, education, and justice. These systems were built under governance traditions that differ from NZ's constitutional commitments — including Treaty of Waitangi obligations. There is no formal mechanism to verify alignment. The gap between "AI aligned with NZ values" and an AI system that demonstrably satisfies NZ's constitutional requirements is unbridged.

### 1.2 The Research Hypothesis

Three philosophical traditions — Māori tikanga, Confucian philosophy, and Western formal ethics — developed in near-complete independence during their formative periods (approximately 500 BCE to 1200 CE for the formative phases of all three). Despite this independence, preliminary LAMAGUE analysis suggests they converged on structurally identical governance constraints across six dimensions:

1. **Human primacy** — governance exists to serve persons, not systems
2. **Earned authority** — legitimate authority is demonstrated through service, not claimed by position
3. **Epistemic honesty** — governance must acknowledge what it does not know
4. **Relational data** — information carries its relational context and the obligations that context implies
5. **Intergenerational obligation** — governance must account for consequences across time
6. **Natural/geodesic path** — right action follows discoverable order, not imposed direction

If these convergences are genuine — not coincidental — they represent the strongest possible empirical basis for universal AI governance requirements: constraints that three civilisations independently validated through millennia of real-world governance experience.

**The research question:** Can LAMAGUE formally demonstrate structural isomorphism between Māori, Confucian, and Western governance constraints — and can AI systems operating under those cross-cultural constraints demonstrate measurably higher governance coherence across cultural contexts?

LAMAGUE (Language of Alignment, Measurement, and Governance for Universal Ethics) is a formal mathematical protocol developed within the Lycheetah Framework for converting governance concepts from any philosophical tradition into computable AI constraints.

LAMAGUE operates through seven symbol classes (Phase Glyphs, Vector Operators, Quantity Markers, Negation/Inversion, Context Brackets, Resonance Markers, Time Operators), achieving substantial compression versus natural language for governance state expression. ## PART 2: METHODOLOGY

### 2.1 Overview

Three-year programme. Three phases. Each phase produces independent outputs.

### Phase 1 — Formal Convergence Analysis (Year 1)

**Objective:** Produce validated LAMAGUE encodings of core Confucian governance concepts and test structural isomorphism with existing Māori and Western encodings.

**Activities:**
- LAMAGUE encoding workshops with Chinese philosophy scholars at partner institution (target: 4 workshops, 2 in NZ, 2 in China)
- Structural isomorphism analysis: compare NZ-derived and China-derived LAMAGUE encodings of the six convergence classes
- CASCADE truth pressure analysis of governance claim sets from all three traditions — test whether convergent constraints emerge as higher-Π content than tradition-specific elaborations
- Draft Convergence Map v2.0 (formalising `CONVERGENCE_MAP.md` with Chinese philosopher input)

**Outputs:**
- Peer-reviewed paper: "Cross-cultural convergence in AI governance constraints: LAMAGUE formal analysis" (target: *AI & Society* or *Ethics and Information Technology*)
- Validated LAMAGUE Confucian encoding set (reviewed and endorsed by Chinese philosophy scholars)
- Convergence Map v2.0 with formal isomorphism analysis

**Method note:** Structural isomorphism is assessed by comparing LAMAGUE operator chains derived independently from each tradition for the same governance concept. Convergence is confirmed when chains share the same primitive operators (regardless of order) at a rate above 80% for a given governance class. Divergence is flagged and documented as genuine tradition-specific differentiation.

### Phase 2 — Empirical Validation (Year 2)

**Objective:** Test whether AI systems governed by cross-cultural LAMAGUE constraints produce outputs rated as more universally appropriate than systems governed by single-tradition constraints.

**Activities:**
- Build three AI agent configurations using `cascade_engine.py` + `aura_sovereign_codex.py`:
  - Agent A: Western LAMAGUE constraints only
  - Agent B: Confucian LAMAGUE constraints only
  - Agent C: Cross-cultural convergent LAMAGUE constraints (all three traditions)
- Cross-cultural governance coherence testing:
  - NZ evaluation panel (60 participants): 20 Māori, 20 Pākehā, 20 Chinese-New Zealanders
  - China evaluation panel (60 participants): mix of demographics across 3 cities
  - Participants rate AI outputs from all three agents on governance appropriateness, trustworthiness, and cultural legitimacy (blind — participants do not know which constraint set each agent uses)
- Statistical analysis: ANOVA for between-group differences; Bayesian analysis for convergence evidence

**Outputs:**
- Empirical dataset: AI governance coherence ratings across cultural contexts (open access)
- Peer-reviewed paper: "Empirical evidence for cross-cultural AI governance convergence: A NZ-China study" (joint authorship NZ-China, target: *Nature Machine Intelligence* or *AI & Society*)

**Ethical note:** All empirical research will receive ethics approval from both NZ (University of Otago Human Ethics Committee) and Chinese (partner institution ethics board) before commencement. Participant data will be anonymised and governed under both NZ Privacy Act 2020 and applicable Chinese data protection requirements.

### Phase 3 — Standard Development and Dissemination (Year 3)

**Objective:** Produce a LAMAGUE Cross-Cultural Governance Standard suitable for bilateral NZ-China adoption and Pacific regional consideration.

**Activities:**
- Draft LAMAGUE Cross-Cultural Governance Standard based on Years 1-2 findings
- Consultation rounds: AI Forum NZ, New Zealand China Council, Chinese AI governance bodies
- Submit to AAAI and Pacific Forum for endorsement consideration
- Policy brief to MBIE, NZIAT, Te Arawhiti, and Chinese counterpart agencies

**Outputs:**
- LAMAGUE Cross-Cultural Governance Standard v1.0 (open licence)
- arXiv synthesis paper: "Constitutional AI Governance Across Cultures: Formal Evidence for Cross-Traditional Convergence" (joint NZ-China authorship)
- Policy brief for bilateral and regional adoption
- Open-source toolkit: LAMAGUE cross-cultural constraint sets for AI developers
