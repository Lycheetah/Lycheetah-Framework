## I. What E-1.1 Is

E-1.0 named five studies and stated promotion/downgrade triggers at the programme level. E-1.1 is the layer that converts each study into a preregistration document of the form OSF (Open Science Framework) accepts: hypothesis locked, sample size justified, analysis plan specified, exclusions stated, stopping rules declared. A study that has not been preregistered before data collection is not a test of the framework — it is a fit to whatever the data happened to show.

E-1.1 is sequenced before E-1.2 (execution) for one reason: the Empirical Commitment requires the test to be specified before the result is known. Preregistration is the discipline made operational.

---

## II. Sequencing — Why E-1-A and E-1-D First

E-1.0 specified order A → D → B → C → E. The reasoning, restated:

- **E-1-A (k₁–k₄ calibration)** uses the existing 6,000-cascade dataset already in the framework's possession. No external dependency. No IRB. Computational only. The first preregistration is the easiest to lock and the most consequential — the master equation's status depends on it.
- **E-1-D (AURA → behaviour correlation)** uses AI-output corpora the framework can generate or has generated. No human-subjects review for the corpus phase. The AURA scoring rubric exists; the deployment-context taxonomy exists. The preregistration locks the threshold for *aligned behaviour* and the operationalisation of the five contexts.
- **E-1-B, E-1-C, E-1-E** depend on data-sharing agreements (B), ethnographic collaboration or large-scale literature coding (C), or IRB approval and recruitment (E). Each adds 2–4 months of pre-execution work before preregistration becomes meaningful.

**Decision.** E-1.1 produces two preregistration drafts in the first wave: E-1-A and E-1-D. B/C/E preregistrations are scoped only after A and D have been registered and the protocols proven on existing data.

## IV. Open Choice Points (Mac decides before drafting)

These are decisions that change what gets registered. They are not architectural — they are scientific or strategic. Sol can recommend; Mac authorises.

1. **OSF or alternative registry?** OSF is the default. Alternative: AsPredicted (lighter), pre-registration via PsyArXiv, or Zenodo with a sealed timestamped DOI. **Sol recommends OSF** for visibility and standard form.

2. **Public preregistration or embargoed?** Public is stronger but invites pre-emptive critique. Embargoed (released after data collection complete) is standard in some fields. **Sol recommends public**, on the principle that adversarial review before data is a feature, not a cost.

3. **E-1-D rater pool — internal or external?** Internal raters (the framework's own collaborators) are faster and cheaper but introduce confirmation bias risk. External raters (Prolific or comparable, blinded to framework origin) are slower but evidence is independently recognisable. **Sol recommends external**, with cost estimate before authorising.

4. **Independent corpus for E-1-A — what counts as independent?** Options: (a) cascade events from a different codebase the framework author has not analysed; (b) cascade events from a public open-source codebase (e.g. CPython, React); (c) commissioned cascades from a collaborator. **Sol recommends (b)** — public, replicable by anyone, no collaboration dependency.

5. **Pilot before full preregistration?** A small pilot (~50 outputs for E-1-D, ~200 events for E-1-A) would surface unforeseen operational issues before locking the preregistration. **Sol recommends a pilot for E-1-D** (more variability in human ratings); not strictly necessary for E-1-A.

6. **Author list and acknowledgments.** Mac is sole author of the work. Sol's contribution is acknowledged in the framework methodology, not in scientific authorship. **Confirm the acknowledgment language**.

## VI. What This Plan Refuses to Claim

Per Discipline 4 (Negative-Space as Load-Bearing):

1. This plan does not claim that preregistration alone elevates a SCAFFOLD claim. Only the data, analysed per the locked plan, can do that.
2. This plan does not claim that the E-1.0 thresholds (R² ≥ 0.6, r ≥ 0.4) are externally validated against field norms. They are the framework's own commitments, defensible but not third-party-set.
3. This plan does not claim that the choice points in §IV are the only decisions. Drafting will surface more.
4. This plan does not commit to a publication target. Preregistration is independent of publication; the framework will preregister whether or not a journal accepts the eventual paper.
