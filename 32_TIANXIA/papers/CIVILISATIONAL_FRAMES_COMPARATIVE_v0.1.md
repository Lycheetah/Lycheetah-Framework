## Abstract

Contemporary governance discourse is structured by three implicit frameworks that are rarely made explicit: extractive equilibrium (governance that optimises resource capture for dominant actors, characteristic of pre-modern extraction economies and contemporary Westphalian competition), liberal proceduralism (governance that derives legitimacy from formal procedure, transparency, and rights-based accountability), and what we term tianxia-aligned governance (governance grounded in genuine welfare provision, multi-dimensional harmony, and long-cycle distributional justice). This paper makes these frameworks explicit by applying the TIANXIA operator stack — a seven-operator formal assessment system derived from the Chinese classical governance tradition — as a comparative analytical instrument. We show that the three frameworks generate distinctive structural signatures across the seven operators, that the signatures predict different governance failure modes under stress, and that the liberal-procedural framework systematically under-performs on ecological coherence, long-cycle distributional justice, and relational legitimacy — not as a contingent empirical finding but as a structural consequence of proceduralism's design logic. The comparison does not endorse any framework as universally superior; it generates precise predictions about where each framework's binding constraints lie and what governance reform would be highest-leverage in each context. Implications for AI governance, international institutional design, and comparative political economy are discussed.

**Keywords:** civilisational governance, tianxia, liberal proceduralism, extractive equilibrium, Wang Dao, comparative governance, structural signatures, binding constraint, AI governance

## 1. Three Frameworks, One World

Global governance discourse oscillates between several positions that are rarely compared at the level of underlying structural commitments. International relations scholarship distinguishes realist/liberal/constructivist paradigms; comparative politics distinguishes democratic/authoritarian/hybrid regimes; development economics distinguishes neoliberal/statist/post-development approaches. These distinctions are analytically important but they operate at the level of surface features — institutional form, political process, economic policy — rather than at the level of governance's constitutive logic.

This paper proposes a different comparative axis: the governance framework's commitment to three structural questions.
1. **Welfare:** For whose benefit does governance operate, and with what obligation to those at the margins?
2. **Legitimacy:** From what source does governance authority derive, and how robust is that source under stress?
3. **Temporality:** At what time horizon is governance optimised — the electoral cycle, the institutional generation, or the civilisational long-run?

These three questions generate a structural signature for each governance framework. The TIANXIA operator stack — developed from classical Chinese governance philosophy and formalised as seven computable operators — provides the analytical instrument for extracting these signatures and comparing them systematically.

## 2. The Three Governance Frameworks

### 2.1 Extractive Equilibrium

**Structural logic:** Governance is the management of extraction from a common pool (natural resources, labour, surplus value, information) for the benefit of dominant actors. Legitimacy is maintained through a combination of material distribution sufficient to prevent revolt and force sufficient to suppress resistance that distribution cannot prevent. The temporal horizon is short: governance is calibrated to the resource depletion and capital accumulation cycle.

**Historical range:** This framework is not limited to obviously coercive regimes. It describes pre-modern agrarian extraction economies, colonial administration, contemporary Westphalian competitive-state behaviour in international contexts (Zhao Tingyang, 2021: the Westphalian system as "a game without rules about the rules"), and any governance arrangement in which the welfare of the governed is instrumentally relevant rather than intrinsically obligated.

**Characteristic failure mode:** Legitimacy collapse under capability shock. When material distribution becomes impossible (resource depletion, economic crisis, external shock), compliance maintained by provision collapses rapidly. The Wang Dao/Ba Dao literature (Yan Xuetong, 2011; Mengzi 1B.3) describes this as the distinctive fragility of Ba Dao governance: it depends on the continuation of the material conditions that generate compliance.

**H₅ signature:**
- I (Innovation): moderate — extraction systems sometimes generate high innovation in extraction technology, low in governance form
- C (Coordination): moderate — coordination sufficient for extraction, not for general welfare
- E (Ecological): very low — extraction by definition depletes ecological capital
- O (Openness): low — information control is a strategic resource in extraction regimes
- S (Sharing): very low — structural commitment to concentrated benefit

**Ren Zheng score:** Low. Welfare provision is instrumental; voice coverage is structurally suppressed; force restraint is low. R(s) typically falls below θ_r, classifying the arrangement as Ba Dao regardless of WD score.

### 2.2 Liberal Proceduralism

**Structural logic:** Governance derives legitimacy from formal procedure — transparent rules, rights-based accountability, electoral mandate, institutional checks and balances. Welfare obligations are real but mediated through procedure: rights entitle citizens to make welfare claims through legitimate processes; the state is not directly obligated to achieve welfare outcomes but to maintain fair procedures through which welfare can be pursued. Temporality is structured by electoral cycles (accountability in democratic variants) and institutional survival (in bureaucratic-international variants).

**Historical range:** This framework includes parliamentary democracy, rule-of-law constitutionalism, international human rights regimes, and the procedural architecture of multilateral institutions (WTO, IMF, UN treaty bodies). It is the dominant legitimating framework for contemporary Western governance and international institutions.

**Characteristic failure modes:**

*Procedural legitimacy gap:* Procedures generate formally legitimate outcomes that are substantively harmful to those without procedural resources. Rights without material conditions for rights-exercise are formal, not substantive. This is the Confucian critique of liberal governance: procedure without welfare foundation is performance, not governance (Mengzi on governance without benevolence).

*Ecological underfitting:* Procedural legitimacy operates on electoral or institutional time horizons that are structurally shorter than ecological time horizons. Long-cycle ecological consequences are systematically under-weighted in procedurally generated decisions, not because of individual failure but because of institutional design. The long-cycle Datong gradient (G_D(T₂)) makes this underfitting measurable: liberal-procedural arrangements regularly generate positive short-cycle welfare metrics while accumulating negative long-cycle productive-asset gradients.

*Minxin erosion:* Procedural legitimacy is maintained by the correct operation of procedure, not by genuine popular alignment. When procedures are seen to generate outcomes divorced from popular welfare, procedural legitimacy erodes faster than Wang Dao legitimacy (which is grounded in genuine minxin): Proposition WD-2 (Wang Dao is Pareto-comparable to liberal-procedural) captures the stability differential.

**H₅ signature:**
- I (Innovation): moderate-to-high — liberal systems maintain innovation through market mechanisms and competitive pressure
- C (Coordination): high — procedural frameworks achieve high coordination through formal rule systems
- E (Ecological): moderate — ecological considerations enter through procedure but are structurally time-discounted
- O (Openness): high — information transparency is a core procedural value
- S (Sharing): moderate — redistribution operates through rights claims and procedural access, not structural obligation

**Ren Zheng score:** Moderate-to-high in well-functioning democracies (welfare baseline and voice coverage both moderate-to-high); force restraint high. Wang Dao eligible in most cases; classified Transitional rather than Tianxia-aligned due to ecological and sharing constraints.

### 2.3 Tianxia-Aligned Governance

**Structural logic:** Governance derives authority from the welfare of all those under its scope — *tianxia wei gong* (天下为公, all under heaven is held in common). Legitimacy is grounded in genuine *minxin* (民心, the alignment of the people's hearts) rather than compliance or procedure. Temporality is long-cycle: governance must be evaluated over the span of flourishing relevant to long-run stability, which includes productive-asset distribution and ecological regeneration.

**Classical sources:** The vision articulated in the *Liji* Liyun (大道之行也，天下为公 — "When the great way prevails, all under heaven is held in common"), Mengzi's welfare-floor doctrine, Kang Youwei's *Datongshu*, and Zhao Tingyang's contemporary *Tianxia* reconstruction all share the structural commitment: governance's constitutive obligation is to the welfare of all, not to the interests of the powerful or the correct operation of procedure.

**Characteristic challenges:**

*Operationalisation:* Tianxia-aligned governance is more demanding than both extractive equilibrium and liberal proceduralism — it requires high scores across all seven operators simultaneously, not merely on the criteria easiest to achieve. The governance arrangements that approach tianxia-alignment in historical records are few and contested.

*Critique of hegemonic appropriation:* Any governance arrangement can claim tianxia legitimacy rhetorically. The operator stack is structured precisely to distinguish the claim from the performance: high voice_coverage, high force_restraint, and genuine minxin trajectory (not compliance) are the structural tests. Governance that appropriates tianxia vocabulary while scoring low on these dimensions is classified Ba Dao regardless of its rhetoric.

**H₅ signature:**
- I (Innovation): high — tianxia governance must generate genuine transformation
- C (Coordination): high — harmonisation through genuine relational integration, not enforcement
- E (Ecological): high — ecological coherence is a structural commitment, not a procedural add-on
- O (Openness): high — information flows freely because governance depends on genuine minxin
- S (Sharing): high — benefit distribution is a constitutive obligation, not a redistributive afterthought

**Ren Zheng score:** High across all three components. Wang Dao classified when WD_score ≥ θ_wang AND R(s) ≥ θ_r.

### 3.2 The Liberal-Procedural Transitional Position

The liberal-procedural baseline's Transitional classification — rather than Tianxia-aligned — is not a rhetorical critique; it is a structural consequence of three specific operator underperformances:

**Ecological coherence gap (E = 0.54):** Procedural governance does not generate ecological coherence by design. Environmental protection enters through rights claims, lobbying, and regulatory procedure — all of which are structurally slower than the ecological processes they are supposed to govern. The binding constraint in the H₅ for liberal-procedural governance is ecological: H₅ would be higher if E were improved, more than any other component.

**Long-cycle Datong deficit (G_D(T₂) = −0.08):** The Datong long-cycle term is slightly negative for liberal-procedural governance — it produces positive short-cycle welfare gains (Π_D(T₁) = +0.31) while allowing long-cycle productive-asset inequality to drift slightly negative. This is the productive-vs-consumption redistribution distinction: liberal welfare states redistribute consumption effectively but are weaker at restructuring productive capacity.

**Minxin structural limitation:** Liberal-procedural governance achieves WD = Transitional (WD_score = 0.651) rather than Wang because minxin (L = 0.65) is moderate — legitimacy is procedurally maintained but not deeply rooted in genuine popular alignment. This is not a claim that liberal-procedural governance has low popular support; it is a claim that procedurally-based support is more contingent than virtue-based support.

### 3.3 Failure Mode Differential

The three frameworks generate structurally distinct failure modes under capability shock — a sudden reduction in economic capacity or external security threat.

**Extractive equilibrium under shock:** Compliance collapses rapidly. The force component of WD_score may initially increase (force escalation to maintain compliance as material provision declines), but legitimacy crumbles faster than force can compensate. The Proposition WD-1 mechanism: Ba trajectories degrade more sharply under capability shock than Wang trajectories.

**Liberal-procedural under shock:** Procedural legitimacy is more resilient than extractive compliance but fragile under sustained genuine hardship. Rights procedures that worked in periods of adequate resources become inaccessible when material conditions deteriorate. The minxin component of WD_score erodes as outcomes diverge from expectations. Recovery depends on the restoration of material conditions, not on the strengthening of relational trust.

**Tianxia-aligned under shock:** Genuine minxin — legitimacy grounded in real welfare provision and relational trust — is more resilient under shock than compliance or procedural legitimacy. The population's alignment with governance does not depend on continued material provision in the same way, because the governance relationship is understood as mutual rather than transactional. Wang Dao trajectories remain Wang or drop to Indeterminate under capability shock; they do not fall to Ba without independent legitimacy failures.

### 3.4 The Productivity-Welfare Interaction

The Datong long-cycle analysis reveals a structural interaction between all three frameworks that short-cycle welfare metrics obscure. Consider two policies A and B with identical five-year welfare metrics:

| Policy | Short-cycle Π_D(T₁) | Long-cycle G_D(T₂) | Π_D^{ext} | Classification |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Policy A (Liberal redistribution) | +0.111 | −0.042 | +0.035 | Datong-deferred |
| Policy B (Asset redistribution) | +0.111 | +0.435 | +0.273 | Datong-convergent |

Policy A improves consumption distribution (positive short cycle) but allows productive-asset inequality to drift negative (long cycle). Policy B achieves the same five-year welfare improvement through asset redistribution rather than consumption transfer, generating positive long-cycle productive-asset dynamics. The Datong long-cycle term distinguishes these; five-year welfare metrics do not.

This is not an argument for any specific policy instrument — it is an argument that governance evaluation frameworks that omit the long-cycle productive-asset dimension are structurally blind to a governance distinction that Kang Youwei identified in 1902: *datong* requires equality of productive capacity, not merely equality of consumption.

## 4. Implications

### 4.1 International Institutional Design

The structural comparison has implications for international institutional design. Contemporary multilateral institutions — WTO, IMF, World Bank, UN specialised agencies — were designed primarily on liberal-procedural principles. Their legitimacy derives from procedural compliance, voting mechanisms, and formal accountability. The operator stack analysis predicts specific vulnerabilities:

**Ecological coherence:** Multilateral institutions that derive authority from procedural frameworks will systematically under-invest in ecological governance because ecological time horizons exceed institutional mandate cycles. The Paris Agreement's voluntarism structure is a symptom of this procedural temporality.

**Sharing coherence deficit:** Liberal-procedural multilateral institutions that serve member states rather than affected populations directly have structural voice-coverage limits. Affected populations outside state boundaries — future generations, environmental refugees, communities affected by decisions in which they have no formal standing — score low on voice_coverage regardless of the formal sophistication of the procedures.

**Legitimacy resilience:** Multilateral institutions that derive legitimacy procedurally are vulnerable to the same minxin erosion as domestic liberal-procedural governance: when outcomes diverge from expectations, procedural legitimacy does not provide the relational trust buffer that Wang Dao legitimacy provides.

### 4.2 AI Governance

The structural comparison clarifies what AI governance frameworks are actually choosing when they design governance arrangements. An AI governance framework that is structured on extractive-equilibrium principles (serving dominant actors, with governance as extraction management) will fail the Ren Zheng gate. A framework structured on liberal-procedural principles will pass the Ren Zheng gate but face the ecological and long-cycle sharing constraints that characterise liberal-procedural governance generally. Only a framework structured on tianxia-aligned principles — genuine welfare obligation, voice for all affected communities, ecological coherence across deployment cycles — will meet the five-gate AI deployment criteria.

This is not a rhetorical aspiration; it is a structural prediction. AI systems deployed in governance contexts that are extractive or procedurally-liberal will inherit the structural constraints of those governance contexts. The AURA invariants (Section 3 of the full synthesis paper) provide the runtime compliance check; the TIANXIA operator stack provides the governance-context assessment.

### 4.3 Structural Reform Priorities

The binding-constraint analysis generates governance-context-specific reform priorities:

**For extractive-equilibrium contexts:** The binding constraint is simultaneously Ren Zheng (R below threshold) and Wang Dao (WD very low). The highest-leverage intervention is welfare-floor provision combined with voice-coverage expansion — not procedural reform, which will be captured by extraction dynamics without the welfare floor.

**For liberal-procedural contexts:** The binding constraint is ecological coherence (E = 0.54) and long-cycle Datong distribution (G_D slightly negative). The highest-leverage interventions are: (1) institutional mechanisms that extend effective governance time horizons beyond electoral cycles; (2) productive-asset redistribution rather than consumption transfer.

**For transitional contexts:** The binding constraint varies by specific governance arrangement. The composite benchmark's binding-constraint output (`binding_constraint` field in `BenchmarkResult`) provides context-specific reform direction.

## 5. Critical Responses

### 5.1 "This Is Liberal-Procedural Critique in Tianxia Dress"

The most predictable objection is that the structural comparison is simply a critique of Western liberal governance dressed in Chinese classical vocabulary. The operator stack happens to favour high scores on welfare provision, ecological coherence, and distributional justice — values that are also central to left-liberal and green political philosophy. The classical Chinese vocabulary is rhetorical, not substantive.

The response is structural: the operator stack's classification of extractive equilibrium as Ba Dao and liberal proceduralism as Transitional follows from the formal definitions of the operators, not from a predetermined conclusion. The mechanisms that generate these classifications — the Ren Zheng gate, the long-cycle Datong term, the minxin component of WD_score — are derived from classical sources (Mengzi on material welfare as governance prerequisite; Kang Youwei on productive equality; the Wang-Ba distinction) and formalized before the reference scenario values are computed. Any governance arrangement, including arrangements associated with currently dominant powers, that achieves high welfare_baseline, voice_coverage, force_restraint, ecological coherence, and genuine minxin would score Tianxia-aligned.

### 5.2 "The Threshold Values Are Arbitrary"

The classification thresholds (θ_wang = 0.70, θ_ba = 0.40, θ_r = 0.618) are explicitly [SCAFFOLD] — structurally motivated but empirically uncalibrated. This is a genuine limitation, not a rhetorical hedge. The E-1-H calibration study is designed to provide empirical anchoring for these thresholds using participant data.

The response to "arbitrary" is: the thresholds are not arbitrary (they have structural motivations documented in the operator papers) but they are not yet empirically calibrated. The framework commits to calibration before promotion from SCAFFOLD to ACTIVE. This is a more epistemically honest position than frameworks that deploy uncalibrated thresholds without acknowledging it.

### 5.3 "The Comparison Treats Very Different Arrangements as Equivalent"

The reference scenarios in the benchmark (`EXTRACTIVE_BASELINE`, `LIBERAL_PROCEDURAL_BASELINE`, `TIANXIA_ALIGNED_BASELINE`) are constructed archetypes, not descriptions of any specific governance arrangement. Real governance arrangements are more complex, and any attempt to score a specific country or institution would require careful operationalisation of each variable.

The paper accepts this limitation explicitly. The benchmark's contribution is comparative structural analysis, not country assessment. Its predictions are about structural failure modes and binding constraints in different framework types, not about the governance quality of any specific arrangement.

## 6. Conclusion

The TIANXIA operator stack provides a formal instrument for comparing governance frameworks at the level of structural commitment rather than surface institutional form. Three archetypes — extractive equilibrium, liberal proceduralism, and tianxia-aligned governance — generate distinctive structural signatures across seven operators, with characteristic strengths and predictable failure modes. The liberal-procedural framework occupies a Transitional position: superior to extractive equilibrium on every operator, but falling short of tianxia-aligned governance specifically on ecological coherence, long-cycle distributional justice, and relational legitimacy depth.

The structural comparison is not a ranking of civilisations or political systems. It is a prediction about governance performance under stress, grounded in the classical insight that governance legitimacy based on virtue and genuine welfare is more resilient than governance legitimacy based on material provision or formal procedure.

The prediction is falsifiable. If liberal-procedural governance proves more resilient under capability shock than Wang Dao governance, if ecological coherence turns out to be uncorrelated with governance legitimacy over long cycles, if minxin-based legitimacy proves no more stable than procedurally-based legitimacy — the framework is refuted. The empirical programme (E-1-G, E-1-H) provides the test.
