## I. Overview

A consistent finding across the TIANXIA operator stack is that minimal intervention tends to produce better long-cycle outcomes than maximum intervention. This finding appears in the Wuwei operator's grain-alignment principle (act at the threshold of necessity, not beyond), in the Wang Dao operator's force-restraint score (governance through virtue costs less than governance through force), and in the Datong distributional gradient (long-cycle equity requires structural reform, not continuous redistributive intervention).

This document formalises the **mutual non-interference constraint structure** — the principle that governs when an agent within a Tianxia governance network may legitimately intervene in the governance space of another agent. This is not merely a diplomatic principle; it is derivable from the first principles of the operator stack. The derivation shows that a Tianxia governance network operating under Wang Dao conditions *necessarily* converges on a mutual non-interference structure — not as an imposed rule but as the equilibrium property of the operator dynamics.

The constraint structure is five-fold, derived from the Wuwei-Tianxia operator coupling. Each component specifies a necessary condition for legitimate intervention. Governance actions that fail one or more components are classified as interference rather than intervention — they violate the Tianxia network's structural requirements regardless of the actor's stated intent.

**Classical resonance:** The five-fold constraint structure resonates with classical Chinese articulations of the conditions for legitimate inter-state action — found in Mengzi 1B.11 (just-cause war doctrine), Xunzi's *Wangzhi*, and the Daoist principle of acting from genuine need rather than preference. The structure is derivable from these sources; it does not require any contemporary source to be named.

## II. Classical Sources

### Wuwei Root Principle — *Daodejing* ch. 2, 17, 48

The Daodejing's formulation of non-intervention is the deepest classical source:

**Chapter 17:**
> *"With the best rulers, the people barely know they exist. Next best are those who are loved and praised. Next are those who are feared. Worst are those who are despised. The best rulers, their words are sparse. When their work is done and affairs are settled, the people all say: 'We did it ourselves.'"*

The governance ideal: presence so light that the governed attribute their own flourishing to themselves. This is not passivity — it is intervention calibrated to necessity, directed with the grain of natural development, withdrawn when no longer needed.

**Chapter 48:**
> *"In pursuit of learning, something is acquired every day. In pursuit of Dao, something is relinquished every day. Relinquish and again relinquish, until non-action is reached. Through non-action, nothing is left undone."*

The Wuwei cascade: iterative reduction of unnecessary action until only necessary action remains. Applied to inter-agent governance: iterative reduction of external intervention until only the minimum necessary for coordination remains. This is the process by which a Tianxia governance network converges toward mutual non-interference.

### Mengzi 1B.11 — Just-Cause Criteria

> *"There are cases where a punitive expedition is justified. If the ruler of a state is a tyrant who causes great suffering, and if the people of that state welcome the punishing army as parents welcoming rain, then a punitive expedition is just. But if the expedition is conducted for the aggrandisement of the intervening state, then it is not just, and the people will resist it."*

Mengzi establishes two necessary conditions for legitimate intervention: (1) the target of intervention has caused genuine harm to its people (not merely to external actors); (2) the intervening agent's motivation is genuinely the welfare of the harmed people (not the intervening agent's own aggrandisement). These are necessary but not sufficient conditions. The operator formalises this structure into five components.

### Xunzi — *Wangzhi* (王制, Regulations of a King)

> *"The true king neither covets the territory of other states nor envies their people. He does not seek to accumulate at others' expense. When his governance is good, other states come to him; when it is deficient, his own territory diminishes. The king does not pursue territory — he pursues virtue, and territory follows virtue."*

Wang Dao governance is defined by *not* coveting external territory or resources. The legitimate governance posture is attraction (virtue draws others) rather than compulsion (force extracts from others). This derives the non-aggression component of the constraint structure.

### *Daodejing* ch. 8 and 81 — Water and Non-Contention

> *"Highest good is like water. Water gives life to ten thousand things and does not strive. It flows in places people reject and so is like the Tao."* (ch. 8)

> *"The sage does not compete, and therefore no one can compete with him."* (ch. 81)

The Daoist articulation: genuine capacity does not require coercion to express itself. A governance entity that must assert itself by suppressing others has insufficient virtue. The mutual non-interference constraint protects this principle: an entity strong enough in virtue does not need to intervene in others' governance spaces to achieve its goals.

## III. The Five-Fold Constraint Structure

Proposition NI-1 (below) derives a five-component constraint structure from the Wuwei-Tianxia operator coupling. The five components are necessary conditions for legitimate intervention:

**NI-1: Genuine Harm (不忍原则, bùrěn yuánzé — The Cannot-Bear Principle)**
Legitimate intervention requires demonstrable genuine harm to the governed population of the target entity — not harm to the interests of the intervening entity, not violation of abstract norms, but harm to actual people that cannot be remedied internally. Source: Mengzi 1A.7 (the heart that cannot bear suffering) and 1B.11.

*Test:* Would a neutral observer, with full information, agree that the governed population is suffering harm they cannot remedy through internal governance mechanisms?

**NI-2: Motivation Purity (无私动机, wúsī dòngjī — Selfless Motivation)**
The intervening agent's motivation must be genuinely the welfare of the harmed people, not the intervening agent's strategic interest or territorial/resource gain. Source: Mengzi 1B.11 explicitly; Xunzi *Wangzhi* (the king does not covet).

*Test:* If the harm to the governed population were fully remedied with no benefit to the intervening agent, would the intervening agent still choose to intervene? If yes: potentially legitimate. If no: motivated by self-interest.

**NI-3: Internal Exhaustion (穷尽内部, qióngjìn nèibù — Internal Remedies Exhausted)**
Legitimate intervention requires that internal remedies have been genuinely attempted and failed, or are genuinely unavailable. Source: Wuwei cascade — intervention is legitimate only after internal pathways are exhausted, i.e., at the genuine threshold of necessity.

*Test:* Have all feasible internal governance mechanisms been given genuine opportunity to address the harm? Has external assistance short of intervention been offered and found insufficient?

**NI-4: Proportionality (适度原则, shìdù yuánzé — Measure Principle)**
The scope and intensity of intervention must be proportionate to the harm being remedied. Intervention must not be used as cover for broader governance restructuring beyond what the harm requires. Source: Xunzi's Li principle — the appropriate form is the minimum that fulfils the function; excess is corruption of Li.

*Test:* Does the intervention scope match the specific harm? Does the intervening entity commit in advance to withdrawal when the specific harm is remedied?

**NI-5: Tianxia Benefit (天下利益, tiānxià lìyì — All-Under-Heaven Benefit)**
The intervention must improve the governance network as a whole — not merely address the bilateral harm while degrading multilateral relationships. An intervention that remedies a specific harm while setting precedents that destabilise the Tianxia governance network fails this condition. Source: Tianxia multilateral coupling (→ W-7) — individual interventions are evaluated against network effects.

*Test:* Would extending the intervention precedent to all similar cases improve or degrade overall Tianxia network stability?

## V. Interference vs Intervention — The Diagnostic

The five-fold structure provides a diagnostic for distinguishing interference (illegitimate) from intervention (legitimate):

| Criterion | Intervention | Interference |
|-----------|-------------|--------------|
| NI-1 Genuine harm | Demonstrated harm to governed population | No such harm, or harm to intervening agent's interests only |
| NI-2 Motivation | Welfare of harmed people | Strategic / territorial / resource interest |
| NI-3 Exhaustion | Internal remedies tried and failed | Internal remedies not attempted; intervention as first resort |
| NI-4 Proportionality | Scope matched to specific harm | Broad restructuring beyond harm scope |
| NI-5 Network benefit | Extends beneficial precedent | Destabilises broader governance network |

An action that satisfies all five is intervention: legitimate under Tianxia governance. An action that fails any is interference: illegitimate under Tianxia governance.

This diagnostic is applicable historically and prospectively. It generates falsifiable predictions: governance entities operating under Wang Dao conditions will systematically exhibit NI-satisfied intervention patterns; governance entities operating under Ba Dao conditions will systematically exhibit NI-violated interference patterns.

## VI. Application to AI Governance

The mutual non-interference constraint applies to AI governance at two levels:

**Level 1 — AI system intervention in user autonomy:** An AI system that intervenes in a user's decision-making must satisfy NI conditions: (NI-1) there is genuine harm; (NI-2) the intervention is motivated by the user's welfare, not the system's objectives; (NI-3) less-interventionist mechanisms have been tried; (NI-4) intervention is proportionate; (NI-5) the intervention respects the broader governance ecosystem (e.g., does not erode user agency as a class). AURA Field Property I (Human Primacy) and V (Reversibility) are the specific invariants this maps to.

**Level 2 — State-level AI governance interventions:** A governance body that restricts another jurisdiction's AI development or deployment must satisfy NI conditions. Unilateral restrictions motivated by competitive rather than harm-prevention concerns (NI-2 failure), applied without evidence of genuine harm (NI-1 failure), or with scope exceeding the specified harm (NI-4 failure) are interference, not legitimate governance coordination.

## VII. Negative-Space Declarations

The mutual non-interference constraint does not claim:

1. **All interventions are illegitimate.** The five conditions define when intervention is legitimate, not that it never is. Genuine harm + pure motivation + exhausted internal remedies + proportionate scope + network benefit = legitimate.

2. **Non-interference is absolute.** The Mengzi just-cause doctrine explicitly permits intervention under the right conditions. The constraint structure specifies those conditions precisely rather than prohibiting all intervention.

3. **The five conditions are unambiguous in application.** Each condition requires judgment about contested facts. The framework provides the evaluative criteria; applying them requires empirical assessment of specific situations.

4. **This constraint structure maps uniquely to any contemporary diplomatic principle.** The five-fold structure is derived from the classical sources (Mengzi, Wuwei, Tianxia) and is applicable to inter-agent governance at any scale. Its resonance with any specific contemporary diplomatic framework is an analytical observation the operator does not make.
